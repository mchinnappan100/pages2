Overview

Aloe (commonly Aloe vera) is a drought-tolerant succulent native to arid regions. Valued for its fleshy, translucent leaves filled with soothing gel, aloe has been used for centuries in traditional medicine, cosmetics, and skincare. It’s easy to grow at home and makes a great houseplant.

Family
Aloaceae
Type
Succulent, perennial
Light
Bright indirect to partial sun
Water
Sparse; allow soil to dry between waterings

Health Benefits

Aloe gel contains vitamins, minerals and polysaccharides. Evidence-backed benefits include:

  • Skin Soothing

    Topical aloe helps soothe minor burns, sunburn and irritation by hydrating and cooling the skin.

  • Wound Support

    May speed superficial wound healing and reduce inflammation when applied appropriately.

  • Moisturizing

    Used in many cosmetic formulas as a lightweight moisturizer and humectant.

  • Antioxidants & Vitamins

    Contains vitamins A, C, E and small amounts of B vitamins and minerals.

Growing & Care

Light

Bright, indirect light is ideal. A few hours of morning sun is fine, but protect from scorching afternoon sun.

Soil

Use a fast-draining cactus/succulent mix or add pumice/perlite to potting soil.

Water

Water thoroughly but infrequently — allow the top 2–3 inches of soil to dry before watering again.

Pot & Repotting

Choose a pot with drainage holes. Repot every 2–3 years when root-bound.

Temperature & Humidity

Prefers 55–80°F (13–27°C). Protect from frost; moderate indoor humidity is fine.

Fertilizer

Feed lightly in spring/summer with a diluted balanced fertilizer (once a month).

Propagation

Aloe is easy to propagate from pups (offsets) — small plantlets that form around the base of mature plants.

  1. Remove pup: Gently remove the pup from the mother plant using a clean knife if needed.
  2. Dry: Let the cut end callus for 1–3 days to prevent rot.
  3. Pot: Plant the pup in a small pot with well-draining soil and water lightly after a week.
  4. Care: Keep in bright, indirect light until established.

Uses

Topical

Gel is applied to soothe burns, sunburns, minor cuts, and dry skin.

Cosmetics

Common ingredient in creams, shampoos, and lotions for hydration and calming effects.

Houseplant

Easy-care ornamental that also improves indoor greenery and air quality.

Culinary & Supplements

Some cultures consume aloe juice or use it in beverages — use caution and avoid latex-containing parts unless prepared properly.

Precautions

  • External use preferred: Skin application of inner gel is commonly safe; avoid using inner gel from store-bought products without checking processing and purity.
  • Oral use caution: Raw aloe latex and some oral preparations can be toxic and cause abdominal pain or diarrhea — consult a healthcare professional.
  • Allergy: Some people may be allergic; test on a small skin patch first.
  • Pregnancy & medication: Avoid oral aloe during pregnancy and consult a doctor if taking medications.

Fun Facts

  • Aloe has been used for thousands of years in traditional medicine across cultures (Egypt, India, Greece).
  • It is sometimes called the “plant of immortality” in ancient texts.
  • Commercial aloe gel is often processed to remove bitter latex components and stabilize the gel.
  • Aloe plants can live for many years with minimal care and occasional repotting.
