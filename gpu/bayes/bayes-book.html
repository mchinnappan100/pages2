Bayes' Theorem

Bayes' Theorem is the mathematical foundation for updating beliefs with evidence. It powers medical diagnostics, spam filters, self-driving cars, and every modern machine learning system. One equation: P(A|B) = P(B|A) · P(A) / P(B). This book builds it from visual first principles to real-world applications.

01

Intuition First

What Bayes' Theorem really says — before any symbols

Imagine you wake up feeling sick. What is the probability you have a rare disease? Your first instinct might be: "I feel bad, so it must be likely." Bayes' Theorem says: not so fast. You need to account for how common the disease is in the first place (the prior), how likely the symptoms are given the disease (the likelihood), and how likely the symptoms are in general (the evidence).

This is Bayesian reasoning: start with a prior belief, observe evidence, update your belief. The updated belief is the posterior. This cycle — prior → evidence → posterior — is the engine of all rational learning.

The Core Loop

Today's posterior becomes tomorrow's prior. Each new piece of evidence refines your belief further. A doctor updating a diagnosis after each test result, a spam filter updating after seeing each word, a self-driving car updating its world model after each sensor reading — all are running Bayes' update loop continuously.

🎯

Prior P(A)

Your belief about A before seeing any evidence. Based on background knowledge, base rates, or previous posteriors.

  • Example: 1 in 1,000 people have disease X → P(disease) = 0.001
  • Called "prior" because it comes before evidence
  • Encodes domain knowledge
  • Can be uninformative (uniform) or informative
🔬

Likelihood P(B|A)

How probable is the evidence B, given that A is true? The diagnostic power of the test.

  • Example: P(positive test | disease) = 0.99 (sensitivity)
  • Not a probability over A — it is fixed, A is fixed
  • Measures how well evidence discriminates A from ¬A
  • The higher the ratio P(B|A)/P(B|¬A), the stronger the evidence
📊

Posterior P(A|B)

Your updated belief in A after seeing evidence B. This is what you were actually trying to compute.

  • Example: P(disease | positive test) = ?
  • Often surprisingly low due to low base rates
  • Becomes the new prior when more evidence arrives
  • The goal of Bayesian inference

Visual Proof

Bayes' Theorem from a Venn diagram — no algebra required

The cleanest proof of Bayes' Theorem needs no algebra — just a rectangle representing the full sample space and two overlapping regions A and B.

Visual Proof of Bayes' Theorem The rectangle = full sample space (area = 1 = total probability) Pink diamond = event A (area = P(A)) Green circle = event B (area = P(B)) Purple overlap = A ∩ B (area = P(A ∩ B)) Step 1: Definition of conditional probability P(A|B) = P(A ∩ B) / P(B) ← "fraction of B that is also A" P(B|A) = P(A ∩ B) / P(A) ← "fraction of A that is also B" Step 2: Solve both for P(A ∩ B) P(A ∩ B) = P(A|B) · P(B) ← from first equation P(A ∩ B) = P(B|A) · P(A) ← from second equation Step 3: They are equal — set them equal P(A|B) · P(B) = P(B|A) · P(A) Step 4: Divide both sides by P(B) P(A|B) = P(B|A) · P(A) / P(B) That's it. Bayes' Theorem follows directly from the definition of conditional probability — no assumptions, no approximations.
The Key Symmetry

Bayes' Theorem reveals a deep symmetry: P(A|B) and P(B|A) are related but not equal. The probability of disease given a positive test (what you want) is not the same as the probability of a positive test given disease (what the test manufacturer measures). Confusing these two is the most common error in probabilistic reasoning — and Bayes' Theorem gives you the exact conversion factor.

03

The Formula

Every form of Bayes' Theorem — discrete, continuous, and expanded

Basic form: P(A|B) = P(B|A) · P(A) / P(B) Named components: P(A|B) = Posterior — belief in A after seeing B P(B|A) = Likelihood — how well A explains B P(A) = Prior — belief in A before seeing B P(B) = Evidence — total probability of B (normalising constant) Expanded denominator (Law of Total Probability): P(B) = P(B|A) · P(A) + P(B|¬A) · P(¬A) Full expanded form: P(A|B) = P(B|A) · P(A) ───────────────────────────────── P(B|A) · P(A) + P(B|¬A) · P(¬A) Odds form (often easier to compute): Posterior odds = Likelihood ratio × Prior odds P(A|B)/P(¬A|B) = [P(B|A)/P(B|¬A)] × [P(A)/P(¬A)] Continuous form: p(θ|x) = p(x|θ) · p(θ) / p(x) where p(x) = ∫ p(x|θ) p(θ) dθ (marginal likelihood)
The Odds Form is Often Easier

Instead of computing P(B) (which requires summing over all hypotheses), work in odds. The likelihood ratio P(B|A)/P(B|¬A) tells you how much the evidence shifts the odds in favour of A. An LR of 10 means the evidence makes A ten times more probable relative to ¬A. Chain multiple pieces of evidence by multiplying their likelihood ratios together.

04

Conditional Probability

The foundation — P(A|B) means "restrict the universe to B, then measure A"

Conditional probability is the single most important concept in probability theory. P(A|B) reads "the probability of A given that B has occurred." It is computed by restricting your sample space to the outcomes where B is true, then measuring what fraction of those also have A true.

Formal Definition

P(A|B) = P(A ∩ B) / P(B), provided P(B) > 0. This is not an axiom — it is a definition that captures the idea of conditioning on information. Once you accept this definition, Bayes' Theorem is just algebra.

Independence

A and B are independent if P(A|B) = P(A) — knowing B tells you nothing about A. Equivalently, P(A ∩ B) = P(A) · P(B). Independence is the special case where Bayes' Theorem simplifies to the prior: the evidence B is irrelevant to A.

Conditional Probability — Restricting the Sample Space Full sample space: 100 people 60 have dark hair (D) 30 have blue eyes (E) 18 have both (D ∩ E) P(D) = 60/100 = 0.60 P(E) = 30/100 = 0.30 P(D ∩ E) = 18/100 = 0.18 P(blue eyes | dark hair) = P(E|D) → Restrict universe to the 60 dark-haired people → Of those 60, how many have blue eyes? → 18 → P(E|D) = 18 / 60 = 0.30 P(dark hair | blue eyes) = P(D|E) → Restrict universe to the 30 blue-eyed people → Of those 30, how many have dark hair? → 18 → P(D|E) = 18 / 30 = 0.60 Notice: P(E|D) ≠ P(D|E) in general → this asymmetry is exactly what Bayes fixes Verification via Bayes: P(D|E) = P(E|D) · P(D) / P(E) = 0.30 × 0.60 / 0.30 = 0.60 ✓

Prior · Likelihood · Posterior

The three ingredients — and how they interact

Every application of Bayes' Theorem involves choosing these three components. The prior encodes what you know before seeing data; the likelihood encodes what the data tells you; the posterior is the result of combining them. Understanding each is the key to applying Bayes correctly.

How Prior, Likelihood, and Posterior Interact Scenario: Is a coin fair? (θ = probability of heads) Prior P(θ): your belief before any flips Option A: Uniform — any bias equally likely P(θ) = 1 ∀θ∈[0,1] Option B: Informative — coins are usually fair P(θ) ∝ Beta(10,10) Option C: Biased prior — you suspect the coin is rigged P(θ) ∝ Beta(1,5) Likelihood P(data|θ): probability of observing 7 heads in 10 flips P(7H | θ) = C(10,7) · θ⁷ · (1-θ)³ ← Binomial likelihood Maximised at θ = 0.7 (the MLE) Posterior P(θ|data): prior × likelihood (then normalise) P(θ|7H) ∝ P(7H|θ) · P(θ) With Uniform prior: posterior peaks at θ = 0.7 With Beta(10,10) prior: posterior peaks at θ ≈ 0.63 ← pulled toward 0.5 With Beta(1,5) prior: posterior peaks at θ ≈ 0.55 ← even more pulled toward 0 More data → prior matters less. Less data → prior matters more. In the limit of infinite data, all reasonable priors converge to the same posterior.

Bayesian Updating

Sequential belief revision — today's posterior is tomorrow's prior

One of the most powerful properties of Bayes' Theorem is that it composes sequentially. You can apply it once with the first piece of evidence, then use that posterior as the new prior, then apply it again with the second piece of evidence. The result is identical to applying Bayes' Theorem to all evidence simultaneously. This means you can update beliefs incrementally, in real time, as new evidence arrives.

Sequential Bayesian Updating — Disease Diagnosis Patient: prior P(disease) = 0.001 (1 in 1,000 base rate) Round 1: Symptom fever observed P(fever | disease) = 0.90 P(fever | healthy) = 0.05 Likelihood ratio = 0.90 / 0.05 = 18 Prior odds = 0.001 / 0.999 ≈ 0.001 Posterior odds = 18 × 0.001 = 0.018 Posterior P(disease | fever) ≈ 0.018 / 1.018 ≈ 1.77% Round 2: Take that posterior as new prior → blood test positive P(+test | disease) = 0.99 (sensitivity) P(+test | healthy) = 0.02 (false positive rate) Likelihood ratio = 0.99 / 0.02 = 49.5 Prior odds (new) = 0.0177 / 0.9823 ≈ 0.018 Posterior odds = 49.5 × 0.018 = 0.891 Posterior P(disease | fever + +test) ≈ 0.891 / 1.891 ≈ 47.1% Round 3: Imaging scan confirms lesion P(lesion | disease) = 0.95 P(lesion | healthy) = 0.01 Likelihood ratio = 95 Prior odds (new) ≈ 0.891 Posterior odds = 95 × 0.891 = 84.6 Posterior P(disease | all 3) ≈ 84.6 / 85.6 ≈ 98.8% Same result as computing P(disease | fever ∩ +test ∩ lesion) all at once. Each piece of evidence multiplies the odds by its likelihood ratio.

The Base Rate Fallacy

Why ignoring the prior leads to wildly wrong conclusions

The most common misapplication of probability is the base rate fallacy: ignoring P(A) — the prior — when computing P(A|B). People focus entirely on the likelihood P(B|A) and forget that a rare event remains rare even with strong evidence.

This is not just an academic concern. It directly affects how we interpret medical tests, court evidence, security screening, and AI model outputs. Bayes' Theorem is the antidote.

The Classic Medical Test Example Disease prevalence: P(D) = 0.001 (1 in 1,000) Test sensitivity: P(+|D) = 0.99 (99% true positive rate) Test specificity: P(−|¬D) = 0.99 (99% true negative rate) → False positive rate: P(+|¬D) = 0.01 Intuitive (wrong) answer: "The test is 99% accurate. I tested positive. Therefore I almost certainly have the disease." Bayesian (correct) answer: Imagine 100,000 people tested: Truly have disease: 100 (0.1%) Do not have disease: 99,900 (99.9%) True positives: 100 × 0.99 = 99 False positives: 99,900 × 0.01 = 999 Total positives: = 1,098 P(D | +test) = 99 / 1,098 ≈ 9.0% Despite a 99% accurate test, a positive result means only ~9% chance of disease. Why? Because there are ~10× more false positives than true positives when the disease is rare (0.1% prevalence). Lesson: The prior (base rate) dominates when it is extreme. No test overcomes a very low prior without very high specificity.
Prosecutor's Fallacy

A related error in legal settings: confusing P(evidence | innocent) with P(innocent | evidence). "The probability of this DNA match by chance is 1 in a million" is P(match | innocent). But P(innocent | match) depends on the prior probability of innocence given the population of suspects — which might be very high. Bayes' Theorem is required to make this conversion, and failing to do so has led to wrongful convictions.

Medical Testing — Full Worked Example

Sensitivity, specificity, PPV, NPV — all derived from Bayes

Medical tests are characterised by four numbers: true positives (TP), true negatives (TN), false positives (FP), and false negatives (FN). All the commonly reported metrics — sensitivity, specificity, PPV, NPV — are conditional probabilities, and their relationships are governed entirely by Bayes' Theorem.

Positive Predictive Value (PPV) via Bayes: PPV = P(D|+) = P(+|D) · P(D) / P(+) = Sens · Prev ───────────────────────────────────────── Sens · Prev + (1 − Spec) · (1 − Prev) Where: Sens = sensitivity, Spec = specificity, Prev = prevalence Example: Sens = 0.99, Spec = 0.99, Prev = 0.001 PPV = (0.99 × 0.001) / (0.99×0.001 + 0.01×0.999) = 0.00099 / (0.00099 + 0.00999) = 0.00099 / 0.01098 ≈ 0.090 → 9.0% PPV Same test, higher prevalence Prev = 0.10 (10%): PPV = (0.99 × 0.10) / (0.99×0.10 + 0.01×0.90) = 0.099 / (0.099 + 0.009) = 0.099 / 0.108 ≈ 0.917 → 91.7% PPV ← same test, very different interpretation!

Spam Filter — Naive Bayes

Bayes' Theorem as a text classifier

Naive Bayes is one of the oldest and most effective text classifiers. It applies Bayes' Theorem to classify a document as spam or ham by computing P(spam | words). The "naive" assumption is that words are conditionally independent given the class — wrong in theory, but surprisingly effective in practice.

Naive Bayes Spam Classifier Goal: given email words w₁, w₂, ..., wₙ, compute P(spam | w₁...wₙ) Bayes' Theorem: P(spam | w₁...wₙ) ∝ P(w₁...wₙ | spam) · P(spam) Naive assumption (words independent given class): P(w₁...wₙ | spam) ≈ P(w₁|spam) · P(w₂|spam) · ... · P(wₙ|spam) P(w₁...wₙ | ham) ≈ P(w₁|ham) · P(w₂|ham) · ... · P(wₙ|ham) Classification rule: If P(spam|words) > P(ham|words) → classify as spam In log space (avoids underflow): score(spam) = log P(spam) + Σᵢ log P(wᵢ | spam) score(ham) = log P(ham) + Σᵢ log P(wᵢ | ham) classify as spam if score(spam) > score(ham) Example: Email: "Win a free prize now" P(spam) = 0.30 P(ham) = 0.70 Word probabilities (from training corpus): Word P(word|spam) P(word|ham) "Win" 0.30 0.01 "free" 0.40 0.03 "prize" 0.35 0.005 "now" 0.15 0.10 log score(spam) = log(0.30) + log(0.30) + log(0.40) + log(0.35) + log(0.15) = -1.204 + (-1.204) + (-0.916) + (-1.050) + (-1.897) = -6.27 log score(ham) = log(0.70) + log(0.01) + log(0.03) + log(0.005) + log(0.10) = -0.357 + (-4.605) + (-3.507) + (-5.298) + (-2.303) = -16.07 score(spam) >> score(ham) → SPAM ✓
Python · Naive Bayes from scratch
import numpy as np
from collections import defaultdict

class NaiveBayesClassifier:
    def __init__(self, alpha=1.0):  # Laplace smoothing
        self.alpha = alpha

    def fit(self, docs, labels):
        self.classes_ = list(set(labels))
        self.log_priors_ = {}
        self.log_likelihoods_ = {}
        vocab = set(w for doc in docs for w in doc)
        self.vocab_size_ = len(vocab)
        n = len(labels)
        for cls in self.classes_:
            cls_docs = [docs[i] for i, l in enumerate(labels) if l == cls]
            self.log_priors_[cls] = np.log(len(cls_docs) / n)
            word_counts = defaultdict(int)
            total = 0
            for doc in cls_docs:
                for w in doc:
                    word_counts[w] += 1; total += 1
            self.log_likelihoods_[cls] = {
                w: np.log((word_counts[w] + self.alpha) /
                          (total + self.alpha * self.vocab_size_))
                for w in vocab
            }

    def predict(self, doc):
        scores = {}
        for cls in self.classes_:
            scores[cls] = self.log_priors_[cls] + sum(
                self.log_likelihoods_[cls].get(w, np.log(self.alpha /
                    (self.alpha * self.vocab_size_)))
                for w in doc
            )
        return max(scores, key=scores.get)

# Quick demo
train = [["win","free","prize"],["meeting","project","deadline"],["free","money","claim"],["report","update","team"]]
labels = ["spam","ham","spam","ham"]
clf = NaiveBayesClassifier()
clf.fit(train, labels)
print(clf.predict(["win","free","money"]))   # → spam
print(clf.predict(["meeting","update"]))      # → ham

The Monty Hall Problem

The most famous counterintuitive result — solved by Bayes

You are on a game show. There are three doors: behind one is a car, behind the others are goats. You pick door 1. The host (who knows what is behind each door) opens door 3, revealing a goat. Should you switch to door 2?

Most people say it doesn't matter — 50/50 either way. Bayes' Theorem says: always switch. Switching wins with probability 2/3.

Monty Hall — Solved with Bayes' Theorem Let Cᵢ = "car is behind door i" (i = 1, 2, 3) Let H₃ = "host opens door 3" Prior (uniform): P(C₁) = P(C₂) = P(C₃) = 1/3 Likelihoods P(H₃ | Cᵢ): P(H₃ | C₁) = 1/2 ← car behind door 1: host can open 2 or 3, picks randomly P(H₃ | C₂) = 1 ← car behind door 2: host MUST open door 3 (can't open 1 or 2) P(H₃ | C₃) = 0 ← car behind door 3: host cannot open door 3 Evidence P(H₃): P(H₃) = P(H₃|C₁)·P(C₁) + P(H₃|C₂)·P(C₂) + P(H₃|C₃)·P(C₃) = (1/2)·(1/3) + 1·(1/3) + 0·(1/3) = 1/6 + 1/3 = 1/2 Posteriors via Bayes: P(C₁|H₃) = P(H₃|C₁)·P(C₁) / P(H₃) = (1/2 · 1/3) / (1/2) = 1/3 ← STAY P(C₂|H₃) = P(H₃|C₂)·P(C₂) / P(H₃) = (1 · 1/3) / (1/2) = 2/3 ← SWITCH P(C₃|H₃) = 0 SWITCH: 2/3 chance of winning. STAY: 1/3 chance of winning. Always switch. The host's action is an informative signal. Intuition: the host's knowledge changes the information content of the reveal. If you had picked from 1,000,000 doors and the host opened 999,998 goat doors, you would obviously switch — the Monty Hall problem is the same logic at scale 3.

Bayes in Machine Learning

How Bayesian reasoning underlies MLE, MAP, regularisation, and beyond

Bayesian probability is not just a tool for updating beliefs — it is the foundation of modern machine learning. Maximum Likelihood Estimation, L2 regularisation, cross-entropy loss, and variational autoencoders all have Bayesian interpretations. Understanding these connections gives you a unified view of the field.

MLE = MAP with Uniform Prior

Maximum Likelihood Estimation finds θ that maximises P(data|θ). This is MAP (Maximum A Posteriori) estimation with a flat prior P(θ) = constant. MLE ignores prior knowledge; MAP incorporates it.

L2 Regularisation = Gaussian Prior

Adding L2 penalty λ‖θ‖² to a loss is equivalent to MAP estimation with a zero-mean Gaussian prior on weights: P(θ) = N(0, 1/λ). The prior penalises large weights.

L1 Regularisation = Laplace Prior

L1 penalty (LASSO) corresponds to a Laplace (double-exponential) prior on weights, which encourages sparsity. The prior places more mass near zero.

Cross-Entropy = Negative Log-Likelihood

Minimising cross-entropy loss for classification is equivalent to maximising the Bernoulli/categorical log-likelihood. Neural network training is MLE.

Dropout = Variational Inference

Dropout at test time (Monte Carlo Dropout) approximates Bayesian uncertainty estimation. Each forward pass with random dropout is a sample from an approximate posterior over weights.

VAE = Bayes with Latent Variables

The Variational Autoencoder optimises the ELBO (Evidence Lower Bound) — a Bayesian objective that balances reconstruction likelihood against a KL divergence prior on latent codes.

MLE vs MAP vs Full Bayes Maximum Likelihood (MLE): θ_MLE = argmax_θ P(data | θ) Ignores prior. Prone to overfitting on small datasets. Maximum A Posteriori (MAP): θ_MAP = argmax_θ P(θ | data) = argmax_θ P(data | θ) · P(θ) = argmax_θ [log P(data|θ) + log P(θ)] Point estimate. Equivalent to regularised MLE. Full Bayesian Inference: p(θ | data) = p(data | θ) · p(θ) / p(data) Maintains the full posterior distribution over θ. Prediction: p(y*|x*, data) = ∫ p(y*|x*,θ) · p(θ|data) dθ The integral is usually intractable → MCMC or Variational Inference In neural networks: MLE ↔ train with cross-entropy, no regularisation MAP ↔ train with cross-entropy + L2/L1 weight decay Bayes ↔ Bayesian neural networks (BNNs), MC Dropout, deep ensembles

Common Misconceptions

What Bayes' Theorem does NOT say

False when prevalence is low. A 99% accurate test on a disease with 0.1% prevalence gives only a ~9% positive predictive value. "Accuracy" conflates sensitivity and specificity into a single number — and neither number tells you what you actually want (PPV) without the base rate. Always ask: what is the prevalence?
False. P(A|B) and P(B|A) can be wildly different numbers. P(cancer | positive test) is what patients need. P(positive test | cancer) is what the lab measures. Confusing them is the foundation of the prosecutor's fallacy, the base rate fallacy, and most statistical misinterpretations in the media. Bayes' Theorem is precisely the formula that converts one to the other.
Mischaracterisation. Priors can be uninformative (flat, maximum entropy), empirical (estimated from data), or conjugate (chosen for mathematical convenience). As data accumulates, the influence of the prior vanishes. Two scientists with very different priors will converge to the same posterior given enough data. The subjectivity of priors is often overstated; the objectivity of the likelihood update is the scientific core.
Not necessarily. Evidence that contradicts your current belief will reduce your confidence. If you believe a coin is biased heads (prior) and you observe 10 tails in a row (evidence with high likelihood under a fair/tails-biased model), your posterior shifts toward fair or tails-biased. Bayesian updating always moves the posterior in the direction of the evidence, which can decrease confidence if the evidence is surprising given your prior hypothesis.
False in practice. The independence assumption is almost never literally true — word frequencies are correlated. But Naive Bayes has proven remarkably effective for text classification, spam filtering, and document categorisation. The reason: for classification (rather than probability estimation), you only need the correct ranking of classes, not accurate probabilities. Even under violated independence, Naive Bayes usually gets the ordering right. It is fast, interpretable, and works well with small training sets.

Book Summary

Bayes' Theorem — P(A|B) = P(B|A)·P(A)/P(B) — follows directly from the definition of conditional probability. It lets you invert a conditional: convert "probability of symptoms given disease" into "probability of disease given symptoms." The prior (base rate) is critical and often counterintuitive: rare events remain rare even after positive tests. Bayesian updating is sequential — each new piece of evidence multiplies the odds by its likelihood ratio. In machine learning, MLE is MAP with a flat prior, L2 regularisation is a Gaussian prior, and cross-entropy is negative log-likelihood. Understanding Bayes is understanding the probabilistic foundation of all of modern AI.
