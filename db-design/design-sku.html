How to Design a SKU Number for Products

Best practices, structure, and tips for building a scalable SKU convention

1. Why SKU Design Matters

A well-structured SKU (Stock Keeping Unit) system helps with inventory tracking, fulfillment, analytics, and product management. Poorly designed SKUs lead to confusion, duplicate records, and operational errors.

2. Define the Key Attributes

Start by identifying the most important product attributes you want your SKU to represent. These might include:

  • Product category or department
  • Brand or vendor
  • Model or style
  • Color, size, or material
  • Variant type (e.g. pack size, edition)

Use a logical and consistent order for these attributes, often from broadest (category) to most specific (variant). :contentReference[oaicite:0]{index=0}

3. Choose a Clear, Concise Format

A good SKU should be human-readable, consistent, and machine-friendly:

  • Keep SKUs short — ideally between 8 and 12 characters (or up to ~16 if needed). :contentReference[oaicite:1]{index=1}
  • Use only letters and numbers; avoid confusing or ambiguous characters like 0 vs O, or I vs 1. :contentReference[oaicite:2]{index=2}
  • Avoid starting SKUs with zero, because leading zeros may be dropped in some systems (e.g. Excel). :contentReference[oaicite:3]{index=3}
  • Use simple separators like dashes (-) or underscores (_) rather than special characters. :contentReference[oaicite:4]{index=4}

4. Build Your SKU Schema

Once you’ve defined important attributes, assign fixed abbreviations or codes to each, and combine them in a structured way. Here’s an example pattern: 

[Category]-[Brand]-[Model]-[Color]-[Size]-[Sequence]

e.g.:

TSH-UA-FLY-RED-M-001

- TSH = T-shirt category - UA = Under Armour brand - FLY = the style or model - RED = red color - M = medium size - 001 = unique sequence number

5. Keep SKUs Unique and Immutable

Each SKU must be unique to a specific product variant. Avoid reusing SKUs across different variants or items. :contentReference[oaicite:5]{index=5}

Once a SKU is assigned, you generally should not change it — changing SKUs can break integrations, reporting, and systems relying on that identifier. :contentReference[oaicite:6]{index=6}

6. Plan for Scalability

Your SKU scheme should be future-proof. Consider:

  • Room for new categories, colors, or models. :contentReference[oaicite:7]{index=7}
  • Using sequential numbering to distinguish new products or versions. :contentReference[oaicite:8]{index=8}
  • Limiting SKU length so it's manageable across systems and people. :contentReference[oaicite:9]{index=9}

7. Avoid Common Pitfalls

Some things to **avoid** when designing SKUs:

  • Using special characters that may not be supported or are confusing. :contentReference[oaicite:10]{index=10}
  • Relying on manufacturer or vendor part numbers — these may not be meaningful for your business. :contentReference[oaicite:11]{index=11}
  • Changing SKU format mid-business process without migration planning. :contentReference[oaicite:12]{index=12}
  • Using lookalike characters (e.g. I, l, O, 0) – they bust readability. :contentReference[oaicite:13]{index=13}

8. Implement Governance & Validation

- Maintain a SKU dictionary or registry so every new SKU follows the pattern. - Automate SKU generation when possible (via scripts or inventory management tools) to avoid human error. - Periodically audit SKUs for duplicates, inconsistencies, or unused items. :contentReference[oaicite:14]{index=14}

9. Real-World Examples

Using a pattern like:

  • HD-SC-SML-PO4: Home Decor → Scented Candle → Small → Pack of 4 :contentReference[oaicite:15]{index=15}
  • 41-VER-FLY-S-001: Category 41, Brand Vera, Style “Fly,” Size Small, Sequence 001 :contentReference[oaicite:16]{index=16}

10. Summary — Key Takeaways

1. Start with broad categories, then add specificity.
2. Keep SKUs short, alphanumeric, and consistent.
3. Avoid confusing or ambiguous characters.
4. Use a reliable SKU generation process.
5. Regularly review and govern your SKU strategy.
