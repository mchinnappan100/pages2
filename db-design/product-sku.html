Why Empty SKUs Are Bad Database Design

A guide to understanding the risks of missing SKU values.

1. Loss of a Unique Product Identifier

SKU values uniquely identify each product or variant. Leaving them empty causes ambiguity, duplicate records, and makes it difficult to link products across systems. Without SKUs, teams often rely on internal database IDs, which are not designed for business operations.

2. Broken Operational Workflows

Warehouse systems, POS terminals, order management tools, and shipping services rely on SKUs for tracking. Missing SKUs can break automations, cause errors in order processing, and force teams to manually resolve discrepancies.

3. Unreliable Inventory Management

Inventory counts are tied to SKU-level tracking. Without SKUs, items cannot be accurately counted, reconciled, or restocked. This results in duplicated products, stock inconsistencies, and potential financial loss.

4. Reporting and Analytics Failures

Sales reports, forecasting models, and product performance dashboards depend on SKU-specific data. Missing SKUs lead to incomplete analytics, inaccurate insights, and flawed business decisions.

5. Integration Problems

Platforms like Shopify, Amazon, eBay, and ERP systems require valid SKUs for syncing products. Empty SKUs cause mismatched items, failed imports, and duplicated entries across external systems.

6. Poor Data Hygiene

Allowing blank SKUs encourages inconsistent data practices. Over time, this leads to messy databases that are hard to maintain, migrate, or integrate with new systems.

7. Violates Database Design Best Practices

Good schema design requires essential fields to be non-null, validated, and unique. Empty SKUs break these principles and compromise the reliability of the entire product model.
