Exercise Stress Test

Treadmill/Exercise ECG — what it is, how it's done, and why it's used

What is an Exercise Stress Test?

An exercise stress test (also called a treadmill test or exercise ECG) measures how your heart responds to the increased workload of physical activity. While you walk on a treadmill or pedal a stationary bike, clinicians record your heart rate, rhythm, and blood pressure to identify signs of reduced blood flow, arrhythmias, or decreased exercise tolerance.

How the Test Works (Step-by-step)

  1. Preparation: Electrodes attached to your chest and baseline vitals recorded.
  2. Exercise: You walk on a treadmill or pedal a bike; speed and incline increase incrementally.
  3. Monitoring: Continuous ECG and periodic blood pressure checks during the test.
  4. End points: Test stops when target heart rate is reached, symptoms develop, or abnormal readings appear.
  5. Recovery: Monitoring continues for several minutes after exercise finishes.

Common Uses

  • Detect coronary artery disease (reduced blood flow to the heart).
  • Evaluate exercise tolerance and functional capacity.
  • Identify exercise-induced arrhythmias or blood pressure abnormalities.
  • Assess response to cardiac treatments and post-heart attack fitness.

Variants of the Test

Stress Echocardiogram

Ultrasound images of the heart before and after exercise to detect abnormal motion of the heart muscle.

Nuclear Stress Test

Radioactive tracer plus imaging to map blood flow to the heart during stress and rest phases.

Pharmacologic Stress Test

Medications used to mimic exercise for patients who cannot physically exercise.

Ambulatory/Exercise ECG

Portable monitors during normal daily activity to capture intermittent issues.

Preparation & What to Expect

  • Avoid heavy meals, caffeine, and tobacco for a few hours before the test.
  • Wear comfortable clothing and sturdy shoes.
  • Continue medications only if instructed by your doctor (some meds may be paused).
  • Bring someone to drive you home if advised (depends on medication use or symptoms).

Risks & Safety

The test is generally safe. Rarely, it may trigger arrhythmia, chest pain, or other complications. Medical staff closely monitor you and stop the test immediately if concerning signs appear.

Interpreting Results — Key findings

  • Normal: No ECG changes, normal blood pressure response, good exercise tolerance.
  • Ischemic changes: ECG changes suggestive of reduced blood flow — may prompt further testing.
  • Arrhythmia: Abnormal heart rhythms provoked during exercise.

Quick Facts

Typical duration
~30–60 min (prep + test + recovery)
Common equipment
Treadmill / exercise bike, ECG monitor
Stopped if
Chest pain, dangerous ECG changes, extreme fatigue

When your doctor may order it

  • New or unexplained chest pain
  • Shortness of breath on exertion
  • Preoperative cardiac evaluation
  • Follow-up after cardiac intervention

FAQ

Is it painful?

No — the exercise itself shouldn't be painful. If you feel chest pain or severe discomfort, let staff know immediately.

Can everyone take it?

Not everyone — alternatives like pharmacologic stress testing exist for those unable to exercise.

Do I need to stop medications?

Follow your doctor's instructions — some medicines can affect results and may be paused before testing.
