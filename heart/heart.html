Heart Stent (PCI) vs Bypass Surgery (CABG)

Overview Comparison Benefits Risks When Recommended Recovery FAQ

Overview

Two common procedures for treating coronary artery disease are Percutaneous Coronary Intervention (PCI) — commonly called a stent — and Coronary Artery Bypass Grafting (CABG), often called bypass surgery. This page summarizes the differences, benefits, risks, and typical recovery to help you understand which approach may be appropriate in different clinical situations.

Note: This information is educational and not a substitute for professional medical advice. Discuss options with your cardiologist and cardiac surgeon.

Quick Comparison

Recovery time 1–2 weeks 6–12 weeks

Ideal for

Single blocked artery, urgent revascularization (heart attack), frailer patients who can't tolerate surgery.

Ideal for

Complex disease (multiple blockages), diabetics with multivessel disease, left main or proximal disease.

Benefits — Stent (PCI)

  • Minimally invasive: smaller access site (wrist or groin), less pain and scarring.
  • Rapid recovery: short hospital stay and quicker return to normal activities.
  • Life-saving in MI: first-line therapy in many heart attacks to rapidly restore flow.
  • Repeatable: can be used for focal lesions; additional stents or repeat PCI possible.

Benefits — Bypass (CABG)

  • Durability: grafts (vein or artery) often provide long-term blood flow around blockages.
  • Better for complex disease: superior outcomes for multi-vessel disease and certain high-risk groups (e.g., diabetics).
  • Treats multiple lesions at once: can bypass several blockages in a single operation.
  • Proven survival benefit: for specific patient groups and severe disease patterns.

Stent Risks

  • Restenosis (re-narrowing), more likely with bare-metal stents (DES reduces this risk).
  • Stent thrombosis — blood clot requiring prompt treatment (antiplatelet therapy crucial).
  • Contrast-related kidney injury (rare), vascular access complications (bleeding/hematoma).

Bypass Risks

  • Longer hospital stay and recovery; higher short-term risk of infection and complications.
  • Stroke risk (small but increased compared to PCI).
  • Pain, scarring from incision; possible sternal wound issues in some patients.

When Each Is Recommended

Stent (PCI)

Focal blockage(s), emergent PCI for myocardial infarction (heart attack), patients with high surgical risk, or when anatomy is suitable for PCI.

Bypass (CABG)

Multiple vessel disease, left main coronary artery disease, diffuse atherosclerosis, diabetics with multi-vessel disease, or failed PCI in some cases.

Recovery & Aftercare

Stent (PCI)

Hospital: 1–2 days. Recovery: 1–2 weeks. Lifelong medication usually includes antiplatelet therapy (e.g., aspirin + P2Y12 inhibitor for defined duration).

Bypass (CABG)

Hospital: 5–7+ days. Recovery: 6–12 weeks or more. Cardiac rehab is strongly recommended to improve outcomes and fitness.

Shared Care
  • Medications: statins, antiplatelets, blood pressure meds as indicated.
  • Cardiac rehab: exercise, education, and risk-factor modification.
  • Lifestyle: smoking cessation, diet, exercise, weight management.

Frequently Asked Questions

Will I need repeat procedures after a stent?

Some patients may need repeat PCI if restenosis occurs; drug-eluting stents and antiplatelet therapy reduce that risk.

Does bypass surgery cure heart disease?

CABG bypasses blocked segments but does not remove underlying atherosclerosis — lifestyle and medical therapy remain essential.

Which option improves long-term survival?

For certain groups (e.g., left main disease, multi-vessel disease in diabetics), CABG has demonstrated a survival advantage. For others, outcomes can be similar when anatomy is suitable for PCI.

How do doctors decide?

A multidisciplinary team (interventional cardiologist, cardiothoracic surgeon, and the patient) considers anatomy (angiogram), comorbidities, symptoms, and preferences to make a personalized recommendation.
